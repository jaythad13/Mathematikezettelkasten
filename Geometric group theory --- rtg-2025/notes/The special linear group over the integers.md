The special linear group and its projectivisation have an important group action on the [[Geometric group theory --- rtg-2025/notes/The hyperbolic plane#_definition _ upper half-plane, hyperbolic plane|hyperbolic plane]].

### Special and projective special linear groups

We should probably give a definition of the special linear group and projective special linear groups somewhere.

##### _definition:_ special linear group, projective special linear group

The special linear group of rank $n$ over a (commutative, unital) ring $A$ is $\mathrm{SL}_{n}(A)$, the group of all $n$-by-$n$ matrices with entries in $A$, and group operation given by matrix multiplication.

The projective special linear group (of rank $n$, over a field $A$) is $\mathrm{PSL}_{n}(A) = \mathrm{SL}_{n}(A) / A^\times$ where the group of [[Abstract algebra --- math-171/notes/Rings#_definition _ unit|units]] $A^\times$ is identified with its embedding as diagonal matrices.

Of particular interest to us are $\mathrm{SL}_{2}(\mathbb{Z})$ and $\mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{Z})$. They are not free (for example the $\pi / 2$ rotation has order $4$), but have very interesting [[Geometric group theory --- rtg-2025/notes/Free groups and presentations|presentations]], that indicate something about their underlying group structure — they are "almost free".

##### _proposition:_ $\mathrm{SL}_{2}(\mathbb{Z})$ is generated by row and column operations

$\mathrm{SL}_{2}(\mathbb{Z})$ is the subgroup of $\mathrm{SL}_{2}(\mathbb{R})$ generated by two matrices corresponding to row and column operations —
$$
\mathrm{SL}_{2}(\mathbb{Z}) = \left< \begin{pmatrix}
1 & 0 \\
1 & 1
\end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix}
1 & 1 \\
0 & 1
\end{pmatrix} \right> \le \mathrm{SL}_{2}(\mathbb{R})
$$
###### _proof sketch:_

Note that the generators, give all integer row and column operations.

Suppose $A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \in \mathrm{SL}_{2}(\mathbb{Z})$, then $ad - bc = 1$, so by [[Superdiscrete --- math-55A/notes/Division and Euclid's algorithm#_theorem _ Bezout's theorem|Bezout's theorem]], $\gcd(a, c) = 1$. Thus, by [[Superdiscrete --- math-55A/notes/Prime numbers#Euclid's algorithm|Euclid's algorithm]], we can reduce the left column by integer row operations and then reduce the whole matrix to the identity —
$$
\begin{pmatrix}
a & b \\
c & d
\end{pmatrix} \to \begin{pmatrix}
1 & b' \\
0 & d'
\end{pmatrix} \to \begin{pmatrix}
1 & 1 \\
0 & d'
\end{pmatrix} \to \begin{pmatrix}
1 & 1 \\
0  & 1
\end{pmatrix} \to \begin{pmatrix}
1 & 0 \\
0 & 1
\end{pmatrix}
$$
Thus, this gives us $A$ as a word in the generators above.

##### _corollary:_ $\mathrm{SL}_{2}(\mathbb{Z})$ is generated by finite order elements

$$
\mathrm{SL}_{2}(\mathbb{Z}) = \left< \begin{pmatrix}
0 & -1 \\
1 & 1
\end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix}
0 & -1 \\
1 & 0
\end{pmatrix} \right> 
$$
where the first generator has order $6$ and the second has order $4$. 

###### _proof:_
follows from change of basis.

##### _corollary:_ $\mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{Z})$ is also generated by finite order elements

$$
\mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{Z}) = \left< \begin{pmatrix}
0 & -1 \\
1 & 1
\end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix}
0 & -1 \\
1 & 0
\end{pmatrix} \right>
$$
or equivalently
$$
\operatorname{Möb} \mathbb{H}^{2} = \left< f, g \right> 
$$
where the first generator has order $3$ and the second has order $2$. Here $f(z) = -1 / (z + 1)$ and $g(z) = -1 / z$.

###### _proof:_

Check that as matrices, the elements to their stated orders are $-I$, and thus, equal to the identity in $\mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{Z})$.

In fact, $\mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{Z})$ is the [[Geometric group theory --- rtg-2025/notes/Free products and amalgamation#_definition _ free product, coproduct of groups|free product]] of two finite cyclic groups.

##### _theorem:_ $\mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{Z})$ is a free product of cyclic groups

$$
\mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{Z}) \cong \left< f \right> * \left< g \right> \cong (\mathbb{Z} / 3 \mathbb{Z}) * (\mathbb{Z} / 2 \mathbb{Z})
$$
where $f, g$ are the Möbius transformations $f(z) = -1 / (z + 1)$ and $g(z) = -1  /z$.

###### _proof sketch:_

Apply the [[Geometric group theory --- rtg-2025/notes/The ping-pong lemma#_lemma _ ping-pong for freely generated subgroups|free product ping-pong lemma]] to
$$
X_{\left< f \right> } = \{ z \in \mathbb{H}^{2} \mid \lvert z + 1 \rvert < 1 \} \cup \{ z \in \mathbb{H}^{2} \mid \operatorname{Re} z < -1 / 2 \}
$$
and
$$
X_{\left< g \right> } = \{ z \in \mathbb{H}^{2} \mid \operatorname{Re} z > 0 \}.
$$

### Fundamental domains of the hyperbolic plane

This result is more than just an example application of the ping-pong lemma. It tells us about the nature of the group action of $\mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{Z})$. It tells us what happens when we try to quotient by it.

##### _definition:_ fundamental domain

A fundamental domain of an [[Geometric group theory --- rtg-2025/notes/Isometric actions#_definition _ isometric action|isometric group action]] $G \circlearrowright X$ is an open set $U \subseteq X$ such that $g \cdot U$ is disjoint from $U$ for each $g$ and $X$ is covered by the union of closures of $G$-tiles —
$$
X = \bigcup_{g \in G} \overline{g \cdot U}.
$$

A fundamental domain of $\mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{Z}) \circlearrowright \mathbb{H}^{2}$ is any minimal (in the sense that there isn't a smaller region inside it also bounded by geodesics) region bounded by geodesics. For example, the region between two vertical lines $x = -1$ and $x = 1$ and above the circle joining $-1$ and $1$ is a fundamental domain.

We can then [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Quotient and identification spaces#_definition _ quotient topology, quotient space, quotient map|quotient]] $\mathbb{H}^{2}$ by $\mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{Z})$ to get an orbifold. A priori, it is homeomorphic to the punctured sphere $S^{2} \setminus \{ * \}$ and thus $\mathbb{C}$. However, because it includes the points fixed by $\mathbb{Z} / 2 \mathbb{Z}$ and $\mathbb{Z} / 3 \mathbb{Z}$, it has additional structure that means the smooth structure of $\mathbb{H}^{2}$ does not transfer to it. In fact, it is an orbifold — a generalisation of a manifold that typically comes from manifolds quotiented by group actions with stabilisers.
