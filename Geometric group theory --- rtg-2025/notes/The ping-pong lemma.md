The ping-pong lemma is an easy way to take a [[Abstract algebra --- math-171/notes/Group actions#_definition _ (left) group action|group action]] $G \circlearrowright X$ and get that $G$ is a [[Geometric group theory --- rtg-2025/notes/Free groups and presentations#_definition _ free group|free group]]. The idea is that if you can guarantee that an element of $X$ is not returned to itself by arbitrary non-reduced words, then $G$ must be free. The most elementary case is that of a group generated by two elements.

##### _lemma:_ the ping-pong lemma

Suppose $G$ is generated by $a, b \in G$ and $G$ acts on $X$. If $X$ has disjoint non-empty subsets $X_{a}$ and $X_{b}$ such that $a^n \cdot X_{b} \subseteq X_{a}$ and $b^n \cdot X_{a} \subseteq X_{b}$ for all non-zero integers $n$, then $G \cong F_{2}$, the free group of rank $2$.

##### _lemma:_ the generalised ping-pong lemma

Suppose $G$ is generated by $g_{1}, \dots, g_{n} \in G$ and $G$ acts on $X$. Suppose further that $X$ has non-empty disjoint subsets $X_{1}, \dots, X_{n}$ and such that for $j \neq k$, we have $g_{k}^n \cdot X_{j} \subseteq X_{k}$ for all non-zero integers $n$. Then $G \cong F_{n}$, the free group of rank $n$.

###### _proof:_

To show that $G \cong F_{n}$, it suffices to show that $G = \left< g_{1}, \dots, g_{n} \right>$ with no relators. That is, the canonical map $F_{n} \cong \left< g_{1}, \dots, g_{n} \right> \to G$ is injective. In fact, it suffices to show this for one element of each [[Abstract algebra --- math-171/notes/The class equation#_definition _ conjugacy classes|conjugacy class]] — $g = 1_{G}$ if and only if $h g h^{-1} = 1_{G}$. Note that by picking a large enough power of $g_{1}$ to conjugate by, every word $g$ is conjugate to some word
$$
h = g_{1}^{m_{0}} g_{k_{1}}^{m_{1}} \dots g_{k_{n}}^{m_{n}} g_{1}^{m_{n + 1}}
$$
where $k_{j} \neq k_{j + 1}$ and $k_{1}, k_{n} \neq 1$. Consider the action of this $h$ on $x \not\in X_{2}$. As we go through each syllable of $h$, $x$ is sent into all the different $X_{k_{j}}$, before finally ending up in $X_{k_{1}}$ and then sent by $g_{1}^{m_{0}}$ into $X_{1}$. One could imagine that if there were only $X_{a}$ and $X_{b}$, $x$ would "ping-pong" between these regions. Thus, $h \cdot x \neq x$ which forces $h \neq 1_{G}$.

Note that we could replace the requirement that the $X_{k}$ are disjoint with the existence of some $x \in X$ not in their union and the requirement that $g_{k}^n \cdot (X \setminus X_{k}) \subseteq X_{k}$ for all $k$. 

Finally the ping-pong lemma can be adapted to prove that a group is a free product. For example, $\mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{Z})$ [[Geometric group theory --- rtg-2025/notes/The special linear group over the integers#_theorem _ $ mathrm{PSL}_{2}( mathbb{Z})$ is a free product of cyclic groups|is a free product]].

##### _lemma:_ ping-pong for freely generated subgroups

Suppose $G$ is generated by some subgroups $H, K \le G$ with $\lvert H \rvert \geq 3$ and $\lvert K \rvert \geq 2$. Suppose there is a [[Abstract algebra --- math-171/notes/Group actions#_definition _ (left) group action|group action]] $G \circlearrowright X$ on some set $X$, and there exist subsets $X_{H}, X_{K}$ such that $X_{K} \not\subseteq X_{H}$.

If for all non-trivial $h \in H$ and $k \in K$ we have $h \cdot X_{K} \subseteq X_{H}$ and $k \cdot X_{H} \subseteq X_{K}$, then $G$ is the [[Geometric group theory --- rtg-2025/notes/Free products and amalgamation#_definition _ free product, coproduct of groups|free product]] $H * K$.

The order hypotheses are important to express words in $G$ as ending and starting in $h \in H$ without loss of generality.
