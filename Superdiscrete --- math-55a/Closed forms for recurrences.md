There is an an easy method to find a closed form for the sequence generated by a linear [[Recurrence problems#_definition _ recurrence relation|recurrence relation]] 
$$
a_n = k_1 a_{n - 1} + k_2 a_{n - 2} + \cdots + k_t a_{n - t}.
$$

##### _example:_ a trivial recurrence

Consider the recurrence
$$
\begin{gathered}
	a_0 = 2 \\
	a_n = 5 a_{n - 1}.
\end{gathered}
$$
We can easily intuit and the prove by induction that $a_n =2 \times 5^n$.

First order recurrences are (almost) always trivial.

##### _example:_ a two-term recurrence

Consider the recurrence relation
$$
\begin{gathered}
a_0 = 1 \\
a_1 = 7 \\
a_n = 5 a_{n - 1} + 6 a_{n - 2}.
\end{gathered}
$$
This is much more difficult to solve, but with some wishful thinking we can believe that there is some solution that looks like 
$$
a_n = c r^n
$$
By manipulating the recurrence we get
$$
r^2 - 5r - 6 = 0
$$
and thus, $r_1 = 2$ and $r_2 = 3$ give values for $r$. Then for each value, find $c_1$ and $c_2$ such that they satisfy the initial conditions.

##### _strategy:_ for solving $t$th order linear recurrences

For a recurrence
$$
a_n = k_1 a_{n - 1} + k_2 a_{n - 2} + \cdots + k_t a_{n - t}.
$$
and some initial conditions for $a_j$ for $j \in \set{0, \ldots, t - 1}$.

1) Solve for the roots of the characteristic polynomial
$$
x^t - (k_1 x^{t - 1} + \cdots + k_t)
$$
2) For $t$ distinct complex roots, find $c_j$ for each $j \in \set{0, \ldots, t}$ solve the linear system given by requiring that the initial conditions are satisfied. If there aren't distinct roots with say $r_1 = \cdots = r_m$, then take $a_n = c_1 r_1 + c_2 n r_2 + \cdots c_m n^{m - 1} r_m + c_{m + 1} r_{m + 1} + \cdots + c_t r_t$.
