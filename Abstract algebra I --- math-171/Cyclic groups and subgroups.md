Cyclic subgroups are the easiest subgroups to deal with — dealing with something like $\left< 3 \right> \le \mathbb Z$ is much easier than dealing with more elements. Here, we will see that when we are dealing with cyclic groups we never need to deal with non-cyclic subgroups. Note that this also makes [[Subgroups#_example _ the lattice of subgroups of $ mathbb{Z} / 6 mathbb{Z}$|calculating lattices of a cyclic group]] easier.

##### _lemma:_ the order of an element divides any "nilpower"

Let $G$ be a group and let $x \in G$ have finite order $\lvert x \rvert = n$. If $x^m = 1_{G}$, then $n \mid m$.

##### _proof sketch:_

By [[Dividing integers - basic number theory#_theorem _ the division algorithm|the division algorithm]], if $n \nmid m$, we would have that the remainder $r < n$ gives us $x^r = 1_{G}$ contradicting that $n$ is the least such number.

##### _theorem:_ cyclic groups have subgroups of all divisor orders

Suppose $G$ is cyclic and finite with $\lvert G \rvert = n$. Then if $d \mid n$ there is a unique subgroup $\left< x^{n/d} \right>$ of order $d$. Further, for any $m \in \mathbb{Z}$, we have $\left< x^m \right> = \left< x^{\gcd(n, m)} \right>$.

##### _proof:_

Suppose we have $d \mid n$.

First, we show that $\left< x^{n/d} \right>$ is a subgroup of order $d$ — we have $(x^{n/d})^d = x^n = 1$, and thus, for $m = \lvert x^{n/d} \rvert$ we have $m \mid d$. But we also have $x^{nm/d} = 1$ and thus, since $n = \lvert x \rvert$ we have $n \mid \frac{nm}{d}$. Since $m \le d$ we must have $\frac{m}{d} = 1$ and thus, $\lvert x^{n/d} \rvert = m = d$.

Now we show uniqueness. Suppose we have some $m < n$ such that $\left< x^m \right> \le G$ has order $d$. Thus, $x^{md} = 1$ and thus, $n \mid md$. Then
$$
\begin{split}
x^m & = x^{(n/d)(md/n)} \\
 & = ({x^{n/d}})^{md/n} \\
 & \in \left< x^{n/d} \right>
\end{split}
$$
Thus, we have $\left< x^m \right> \subseteq \left< x^{n/d} \right>$ and since they have the same order, we have equality.

Suppose we have $m \in \mathbb{Z}$. Note that we have $\left< x^m \right> \subset \left< x^{\gcd(n, m)} \right>$ since $(x^m)^k = (x^{\gcd(n,m)})^{bk}$ where $b = \frac{m}{\gcd(n, m)}$.

##### _example:_ what's the subgroup lattice of $\mathbb{Z} / 24 \mathbb{Z}$?

We can see that this theorem allows us to easily write the subgroup lattices of a cyclic group. The subgroup lattice for the cyclic group of order $n$ is just the dual category to the divides by poset on $\{ 0, \dots, n \}$.

### Subgroups generated by subsets of a group

A natural extension of cyclic subgroups of a group, is the subgroup generated by some subset of a group $G$.

##### _definition:_ the subgroup generated by a subset

If $A$ is any subset of a group $G$, then we say the subgroup of $G$ generated by $A$ is
$$
\left< A \right> = \bigcap_{H \leq G, A \subset H} H 
$$

Note that this is "the smallest subgroup containing $A$".

##### _theorem:_ the subgroup generated by $A$ is the subgroup generated by the elements in $A$

$$
\left< A \right> = \left< a \in A \right>.
$$
