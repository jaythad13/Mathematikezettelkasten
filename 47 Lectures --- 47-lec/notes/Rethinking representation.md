We will talk about what representation is, and how mathematics can help with it, beyond just redistricting.

The typical problems with democracy, especially representative democracy are
1) How can a smaller group represent everyone?
2) How do we secure minority voice while preserving majority rule?

We can critique/defend systems based on these principles, but also, we can consider how we could design systems that live up to these principles.

### What is representation?

We want representation to be an _image_, or _portrait_ of the people of the country: to quote John Adams, "it should think, feel, reason, and act like them". 

What does this mean to a mathematician? Typically, in math, we look at various systems and prove properties of them.

What does it mean to a language philosopher? They look at questions of accountability of representatives, who they should represent, et c.

What does it mean to a civil rights scholar? They look at questions of effectiveness - how effective is representation at achieving its goals.

### How do people vote? What mathematicians think over the years

We need some way to take the preferences of everyone and turn them into a "representative" opinion.

Systems matter for this! (see Burlington, Vermont mayoral race).

##### _question:_ Aren't Condercet candidates always just center-ish candidates?

In the 19th century, people were thinking about simpler problems like this:

##### _theorem:_ Condorcet jury theorem

If each voter makes a correct decision with probability $p > 1/2$, then a decision by a large group is very likely correct. 

In the mid-20th century, Ken Arrow proves a bunch of theorems about how if you want enough nice properties, there is no voting system that satisfies all of them.

In the 90s, people started noticing similarities between computational problems and problems in voting.

### Solutions to gerrymandering

The first solution: check everything. This is intractable.

The second solution: optimise. But for what? And also, computationally intractable.

The third solution: sample! This works, but is risky because people often misunderstand it.

The fourth solution: test, test, and retest!

The fifth solution: STV just beats gerrymandering every time.

We still need to care about districts because:
- it's not nearly politically feasible to switch to STV everywhere
- voters often prefer to vote in neighbourhood structures, to "capture shared needs", at least at more local levels
- STV leads to less majoritarian governments which might be more representative, but can definitely be less effective (think Atal Bihari Vajpayee, et c.)
