Let $X$ be a smooth manifold.

The idea of Morse theory is to generalise the height functions to functions that subdivide the manifold into nice level sets. Because we have observed the behaviour change at critical points, the right idea is to consider functions with isolated (discrete) critical points.

##### _definition:_ non-degenerate, Morse type critical points, maxima, minima

A [[UChicago --- uc-2026/notes/Manifolds background#_definition _ critical point|critical point]] of a smooth function $f : X \to \mathbb{R}$ is **non-degenerate** or of **Morse type** if its Hessian has non-zero determinant.

---

For these nice critical points, we can define an index. This is basically the [[Quadratic forms and K-theory --- pcmi-2021/notes/Diagonalisation of quadratic forms#_corollary _ diagonalising quadratic forms over $ overline{ mathbb{F}}$ and $ mathbb{R}$|signature]] of the quadratic form defined by the symmetric matrix that is the Hessian of $f$.

##### _definition, proposition:_ index of a Morse critical point on $\mathbb{R}^n$

Given $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ with a Morse critical point at $0$ there is a unique integer $s$ and a change of coordinates $\varphi : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ such that $f \circ \varphi = - \sum_{i = 1}^s x_{i}^{2} + \sum_{i = s + 1}^n x_{i}^{2}$. 

Equivalently, $s$ is the number of negative eigenvalues of the Hessian.

This unique integer $s$ is the **index** of the critical point of $f$ at $0$.

##### _lemma:_ classifying Morse behaviour at critical points by index

Suppose $X$ is a smooth compact surface and $f : X \to \mathbb{R}$ is a smooth function. Let $f(p) = z$.

Suppose $p$ is an index $0$ critical point of $f$. Then, for small enough $\varepsilon > 0$, $f^\text{pre}(-\infty, z + \varepsilon)$ has one more connected component than $f^\text{pre}(-\infty, z - \varepsilon)$. That is,
$$
\# \pi_{0}(f^\text{pre}(-\infty, z + \varepsilon)) = 1 + \# \pi_{0}(f^\text{pre}(-\infty, z - \varepsilon)).
$$

Suppose $p$ is an index $1$ critical point of $f$. Then, for small enough $\varepsilon > 0$ either 
- there is a $1$-cycle in $f^\text{pre}(z - \varepsilon)$ that is not homologous to any cycle in $f^\text{pre}(-\infty, z - \varepsilon)$, or
- $\# \pi_{0}(f^\text{pre}(-\infty, z + \varepsilon)) = \# \pi_{0}(f^\text{pre}(-\infty, z - \varepsilon)) - 1$.

Suppose $p$ is an index $2$ critical point of $f$. Then, for small enough $\varepsilon > 0$ either
- there is a null-homologous $1$-cycle in $f^\text{pre}(-\infty, z - \varepsilon)$ that is trivial in $f^\text{pre}(-\infty, z + \varepsilon)$, or
- there are no null-homologous $1$-cycles in $f^\text{pre}(-\infty, z - \varepsilon)$ and it has one more *closed* (boundaryless) connected component than $f^\text{pre}(-\infty, z + \varepsilon)$.
