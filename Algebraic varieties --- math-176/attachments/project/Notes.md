#### No cheats

For example, Bezout's theorem requires lots of weasel words

##### _theorem:_ Bezout's theorem

*In general* if two plane curves have no component in common, then *counting with multiplicity* (in projective space over an algebraically closed field), they intersect at as many points as the product of their degree.

Or even the fundamental theorem of algebra requires *counting with multiplicity*.

#### Connections to deep math

In the direction of enumerative geometry and intersection theory lie results like the fact that a general quintic hypersurface in $\mathbb{P}^4$ contains $2875$ lines, there are $3264$ conics tangent to five general conics, and that there are $5,819,539,783,680$ twisted cubics tangent to $12$ quadrics. Of course it isn't exactly the numbers that matter, but the fact that we can find these numbers exactly!

Parameter spaces are tools that allow us to understand all varieties of a certain type by varying just a few parameters. These parameter spaces often have a geometry of their own that respects the geometry of the varieties we're thinking about.

##### _example:_ the parameter space of circles

The parameter space of circles in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is $\mathbb{R}^2 \times \mathbb{R}^+$ where the first part represents the centre and the second represents the radius.

More generally, at the cutting edge of math is research on moduli spaces, which are more general "coordinate-free" versions of these parameter spaces that can be used to solve geometric classification problems (and more enumerative problems like these).

Birational geometry is also a field of current interest in algebraic geometry — the minimal model programme and classification of surfaces upto birational equivalence which is like isomorphism on a dense subset.

Finally blowups are something we've seen before! They're really powerful because they allow you to see things that look singular as smooth in a higher-dimensional space.
