In order to do homology we typically need [[Abstract Algebra I --- math-171/notes/Groups, and why you should care#_definition _ abelian group, commutative group|abelian groups]]. We can use vector spaces!

##### _definition:_ free vector space

Let $B$ be any set and let $\mathbb{F}$ be a field. The free vector space $\mathbb{F}^{(B)}$ is the [[Linear algebra done right --- ladr/notes/Vector spaces#_definition _ vector space|vector space]] of functions $B \to \mathbb{F}$ with finite support (for $f \in \mathbb{F}^{(B)}$ there are only finitely many $b \in B$ with $f(b) \neq 0$).

Note that we can identify the basis of this vector space with $B$ — $b \in B$ is identified with $\hat{b} : b \mapsto 1$ and $\hat{b} : a \mapsto 0$ for any other $a \in B$. These clearly form a [[Linear algebra done right --- ladr/notes/Bases#|basis]].

By abuse of notation, we will often write $b$ in place of $\hat{b}$.

##### _example:_ cycles over fields

The reason we even defined this is that [[Topics in Geometry and Topology --- math-145/notes/Cycles over arbitrary rings#_definition _ $1$-chains, $1$-cycles over $A$|a cycle over a field]] $\mathbb{F}$ (in a subset $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^{2}$) is just the free vector space $\mathbb{F}^{(B)}$ where $B$ is the set of line segments in the plane. We often write this $\mathrm{C}_{1}(U, \mathbb{F})$.

### Chains in the plane

Chains are built out of basic geometric components.

##### _definition:_ point

A point is $[\mathbf{a}]$ for some $\mathbf{a} \in \mathbb{R}^{2}$. It has support $\{ \mathbf{a} \}$.

![[Topics in Geometry and Topology --- math-145/notes/Discrete winding number#_definition _ line segment, support of a line segment|Discrete winding number]]

##### _definition:_ directed triangles

A directed triangle $[\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{c}]$ is its support $\{ s \mathbf{a} + t \mathbf{b} + u \mathbf{c} \mid s + t + u = 1 \text{ and } s, t,u \ge 0 \}$ and the information of their order.

##### _definition:_ $0$, $1$, and $2$-chains

Suppose $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^{2}$ and $\mathbb{F}$ is a [[Abstract Algebra I --- math-171/notes/Fields#_definition _ fields|field]].

The vector space of $0$-chains in $U$ over $\mathbb{F}$ is $\mathrm{C}_{0}(U, \mathbb{F})$ and is the free vector space of points over $\mathbb{F}$.

The vector space of $1$-chains in $U$ over $\mathbb{F}$ is $\mathrm{C}_{1}(U, \mathbb{F})$ and is the free vector space of (directed) line segments over $\mathbb{F}$.

The vector space of $2$-chains in $U$ over $\mathbb{F}$ is $\mathrm{C}_{2}(U, \mathbb{F})$ and is the free vector space of directed triangles over $\mathbb{F}$.

We could quotient out so that $[\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}] = - [\mathbf{b}, \mathbf{a}]$ and more generally so that $[\mathbf{a}_{\tau(1)}, \dots, \mathbf{a}_{\tau(n)}] = (-1)^{\operatorname{sgn} \tau}$ — quotienting out by the [[Abstract Algebra I --- math-171/notes/Group combinatorics and symmetric groups#_proposition _ $S_{n}$ is generated by all the obvious transpositions|sign]] of each permutation. In fact, most of the maps we define will factor through a vector space like this. Nonetheless, we won't bother doing the quotienting.

### $1$-chains

The boundary map on $1$-chains does the natural thing we might expect it to.

##### _definition:_ boundary of a $1$-chain

The boundary map $\partial_{0} : \mathrm{C}_{1} \to \mathrm{C}_{0}$ is the [[Linear maps|linear map]] defined by extending $\partial_{0} : [\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}] \mapsto [\mathbf{b}] - [\mathbf{a}]$ linearly.

##### _example:_ boundaries of some chains

Thus, the boundary of a $1$-[[Topics in Geometry and Topology --- math-145/notes/Cycles over arbitrary rings#_definition _ $1$-chains, $1$-cycles over $A$|cycle]] $\sum_{i = 1}^n \lambda_{i} [\mathbf{a}_{i}, \mathbf{b}_{i}]$ is $0$ and the boundary of a $1$-chain is $\sum_{i = 1}^n \lambda_{i} [\mathbf{a}_{i}, \mathbf{b}_{i}]$.

In fact, being a $1$-cycle is the same thing as being in the kernel of $\partial_{0}$ which is very nice — a natural thing to do with 

##### _proposition:_ cycles are the kernel of the boundary map

A $1$-chain $\gamma \in \mathrm{C}_{1}(U, \mathbb{F})$ is in $\ker \partial_{0}$ if and only if $\gamma$ is a $1$-cycle.

###### _proof:_

For $\gamma = \sum_{i = 1}^n [\mathbf{a}_{i}, \mathbf{b}_{i}]$ to be a one cycle is for
$$
\sum_{i \mid \mathbf{a}_{i} = \mathbf{v}} \lambda_{i} = \sum_{i \mid \mathbf{b}_{i} = \mathbf{v}} \lambda_{i}.
$$

But
$$
\begin{align}
\partial_{0} \gamma & = \sum_{i = 1}^n \lambda ([\mathbf{a}_{i}] - [\mathbf{b}_{i}]) \\
 & = \left( \sum_{i \mid \mathbf{a}_{i} = \mathbf{v}} \lambda_{i} - \sum_{i \mid \mathbf{b}_{i} = \mathbf{v}} \lambda_{i}  \right)[\mathbf{v}]. \\
 & = 0.
\end{align}
$$
One can prove the other direction exactly by reversing these steps.

##### _definition:_ $1$-cycles

The space of $1$-cycles is $\mathrm{Z}_{1}(U, \mathbb{F}) = \ker \partial_{0}$.

The other natural thing o do with a linear map is to look at its image. In particular, we ask when we can solve for $\gamma \in \mathrm{C}_{1}(U, \mathbb{F})$ such that $\partial_{0} \gamma = \alpha$ (for some point $\alpha \in \mathrm{C}_{0}(U, \mathbb{F})$).

##### _definition:_ $0$-boundaries

The space of $0$-boundaries is the space of all $0$-chains in $0$ that are the boundary of some $1$-chain in $U$
$$
\mathrm{B}_{0}(U, \mathbb{F}) = \operatorname{img} \partial_{0}.
$$

If you look at enough examples in [[Topics in Geometry and Topology --- math-145/attachments/texts/MATH 145 notes.pdf#page=36|examples in the notes]] we see that this should only possible if in every [[Mathematical Analysis I --- math-131/notes/Connectedness#_definition _ connectedness|connected]] component of $U$ the sums of coefficients is zero. It's not hard to see that this is necessary If you stare longer you will see that it's sufficient too.

This motivates the definition of a linear map to keep track of this.

##### _definition:_ the mass cocycle

The mass cocycle is a [[Dual spaces|dual map]] $m \in \mathrm{C}_{0}^\vee(U, \mathbb{F})$ is defined by $m : \sum \lambda_{i} [\mathbf{a}_{i}] = \sum \lambda_{i}$.

Now we can prove that it's really true.

##### _proposition:_ equivalent condition to be a boundary

A $0$-chain $\alpha = \sum_{i = 1}^n \lambda_{i} [\mathbf{a}_{i}]$ is a boundary in $\mathrm{B}_{0}(U, \mathbb{F})$ if and only if $m(\alpha_{|U}) = 0$ on each connected component $V$.

Here the restriction of $\alpha$ to $V$ is denoted $\alpha_{|V}$.

###### _proof:_

Note that $m(\partial_{0}[\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}]) = m([\mathbf{b}] - [\mathbf{a}]) = 0$. Thus, we require $m(\alpha) = 0$. Since [[Complex Analysis --- math-135/notes/Analysis and (metric) topology review#_proposition _ path-connectedness and connectedness are equivalent on open sets|path-connectedness and connectedness are equivalent]] for a subset of $\mathbb{R}^{2}$, each polygonal path between $[\mathbf{a}]$ and $[\mathbf{b}]$ with boundary $[\mathbf{b}] - [\mathbf{a}]$ must be within a connected component $V$. Thus, we also require $m(\alpha_{|V}) = 0$. 

Proving that $m(\alpha_{|V}) = 0$ is sufficient is [[Topics in Geometry and Topology --- math-145/attachments/homework/hw 3/hw 4.pdf|homework]].

### $2$-chains

Here we're going to run into trouble for not defining $[\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}] = -[\mathbf{b}, \mathbf{a}]$.

##### _definition:_ boundary of a $2$-chain

The boundary map on $2$-chains is the linear map $\partial_{1} : \mathrm{C}_{2}(U, \mathbb{F}) \to \mathrm{C}_{1}(U, \mathbb{F})$ defined by extending $\partial_{1} : [\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{c}] \mapsto [\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}] + [\mathbf{b}, \mathbf{c}] - [\mathbf{c}, \mathbf{a}]$ linearly.

There isn't really much to be said about $2$-cycles. What about $1$-boundaries? That is, when can we find a $2$-chain $\sigma$ such that a given $\gamma$ is its boundary?

##### _definition:_ $1$-boundaries

The space of $1$-boundaries is the space of all $1$-chains in $U$ that are the boundary of some $2$-chain in $U$
$$
\mathrm{B}_{1}(U, \mathbb{F}) = \operatorname{img} \partial_{1}.
$$

##### _proposition:_ the boundary of a $2$-chain is a cycle

The boundary of a $2$-chain must be a $1$-cycle. Equivalently $\partial_{0} \partial_{1} = 0$. Equivalently $\mathrm{B}_{1} \le \mathrm{Z}_{1}$.

###### _proof sketch:_

The $2$-chains are generated by triangles. Check that the boundary of the boundary of a triangle is zero.

This leads us to ask "which $1$-cycles are boundaries of $2$-chains?"

In the case of $1$-chains, as long as $U$ was connected we had that a $0$-cycle is indeed a boundary. Otherwise [[Topics in Geometry and Topology --- math-145/attachments/homework/hw 4/hw 4.pdf|the homework]] shows us that the zeroth homology group $\mathrm{H}_{0} = \mathrm{Z}_{0} / \mathrm{B}_{0}$ measures the number of connected components. Here again, the homology group measures the degree of obstruction to $1$-cycles being boundaries of $2$-chains

##### _definition:_ first homology group

The first homology group on $U$ over $\mathbb{F}$ is the [[Quotient spaces|quotient vector space]]
$$
\mathrm{H}_{1}(U, \mathbb{F}) = \mathrm{Z}_{1} / \mathrm{B}_{1}.
$$

Specifically, what does the homology measure? Whatever it is, the [[Topics in Geometry and Topology --- math-145/notes/Discrete winding number#_definition _ winding number (of a $1$-cycle)|winding number]] gives a way to measure it too — specifically, if a $1$-cycle is the boundary of a $2$-chain, it has winding number $0$ about every point not in the support of the $2$-chain.

##### _proposition:_ the winding number of the boundary of a chain

Given $x$ not in the support of a $2$-chain $\sigma$, the winding number of $\partial_{1} \sigma$ about $x$ is $0$.

###### _proof sketch:_

See by the [[Topics in Geometry and Topology --- math-145/attachments/homework/hw 1/hw 1.pdf#page=2|distant cycle lemma]] that this is true for any $[\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{c}]$. Since $w_{x} : \gamma \mapsto w(\gamma, x)$ is linear, this extends linearly to the zero function.
