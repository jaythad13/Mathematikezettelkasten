### Lagrange's theorem

One nice application of the idea of [[Abstract algebra --- math-171/notes/Fibres and quotients#_definition _ cosets|cosets]].

##### _theorem:_ Lagrange's theorem

If $G$ is a finite group and $H \le G$, then $\lvert H \rvert \mid \lvert G \rvert$ and the number of left (and right) cosets of $H$ in $G$ is $\lvert G \rvert / \lvert H \rvert$.

###### _proof sketch:_

We've already shown this [[Abstract algebra --- math-171/attachments/homework/hw 7/hw 7.pdf#page=5|in the homework]], just in the language of group actions. Just think of the orbit of $g$ with respect to $H$ as the coset of $g$ with respect to $H$.

This has two neat corollaries

##### _corollary:_ every element is $\lvert G \rvert$-nilpotent

If $G$ is a finite group and $x \in G$ then $\lvert x \rvert \mid \lvert G \rvert$ and $x^{\lvert G \rvert} = 1$ for all $x \in G$.

###### _proof:_

Consider the cyclic subgroup generated by $x$. Since $\lvert \left< x \right> \rvert = \lvert x \rvert$ and $\lvert \left< x \right> \rvert \mid \lvert G \rvert$ (by [[#_theorem _ Lagrange's theorem|Lagrange's theorem]]) we have $\lvert x \rvert  \mid \lvert G \rvert$. Thus, $x^{\lvert G \rvert} = 1$.

##### _corollary:_ all groups of prime order are cyclic

If $\lvert G \rvert = p$ for some prime $p$, then $G \cong \mathbb{Z} /p\mathbb{Z}$.

###### _proof:_

For any (non-identity) $x$, we know (from the previous result) that $\lvert x \rvert \mid \lvert G \rvert$. But then $\lvert x \rvert \mid p$ for prime $p$. Since $\lvert x \rvert \neq 1$, we have $\lvert x \rvert = p = \lvert G \rvert$. Thus, $G$ is cyclic and of order $p$. Since all cyclic groups of the same order are isomorphic, we have $G \cong \mathbb{Z} / p \mathbb{Z}$.

### Indices

It can be useful to know how many cosets of a subgroup there are (we will see a result that really cements this). Thus, we define

##### _definition:_ index, $\lvert G : H \rvert$

Let $G$ be a group and $H$ be a subgroup. Then, the number of left (and right) cosets of $H$ in $G$ is the index of $H$ in $G$, denoted by $\lvert G : H \rvert$.

We see the usefulness of this by

##### _proposition:_ index $2$ subgroups are normal

If $H \le G$ and $\lvert G : H  \rvert = 2$, then $H \trianglelefteq G$.

###### _proof:_

Note that there must be exactly $2$ left cosets and exactly $2$ right cosets. For each one of them has to be $H$ itself, and thus, the others are just $gH$ and $Hg$ for $g \not\in H$. Note that since the cosets partition the group, we have $gH = G \setminus H = Hg$ for $g \not\in H$. For $g \in H$ obviously $gH = H = Hg$.

Thus for every $g \in G$ we have $gH = Hg$ and thus, $g H g^{-1} = H$.

### Partial converses

We've shown that the [[Abstract algebra --- math-171/notes/Cyclic groups and subgroups#_theorem _ cyclic groups have subgroups of all divisor orders|converse of this theorem is true for cyclic groups]]. While this is not generally true for all groups, we can get partial converses of this theorem. It turns out that a partial converse is powerful because it allows you to create groups of various orders out of already existing groups.

##### _counterexample:_ the converse of Lagrange's theorem is false

Consider $A$ the group of rotational symmetries (about any vertex) of the regular tetrahedron. Note that $\lvert A \rvert = 12$. Then there is no subgroup $H$ of order $6$ (even though $6 \mid 12$).
