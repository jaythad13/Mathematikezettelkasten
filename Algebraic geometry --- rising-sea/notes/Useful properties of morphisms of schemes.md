Let $X, Y$ be a schemes.

Grothendieck taught us that [[Algebraic geometry --- rising-sea/notes/Morphisms of schemes#_definition _ morphism of schemes|morphisms]] are more fundamental than objects. Specifically, he taught us that a property of schemes can be turned into a property of morphisms. Often we will say $\pi : X \to Y$ has a property if $\pi^\text{pre}(U)$ has that property for each affine open $U \subseteq Y$. Eventually when we understand fibres of a morphism as fibred products of schemes, we will see that we can think of this as each fibre having the property and the property behaving well as the fibre varies.

We will also define what it means for a property to be "reasonable". Though not all useful properties will be reasonable, often that is because they need to be fixed.
