Recall the notion of a metric space — the motivation for considering topological spaces at all.

![[Mathematical Analysis I --- math-131/notes/Metric spaces#_definition_ metric space, metric|Metric spaces]]

The metric space [[Mathematical Analysis I --- math-131/notes/Open and closed sets|has a topology]] generated by open balls. These are very nice topological spaces — they satisfy every reasonable separation property and play nicely with size restrictions and covering properties. Finally, they can always be embedded in really nice spaces.

### Separation, size, and covering

Metric spaces satisfy all separation properties from $T_{1}$ to $T_{4}$ and some more that we haven't explored (all the way up to $T_{6}$).

##### _proposition:_ metric spaces are Hausdorff, regular, and normal

Any metric space $X$ is $T_{1}$, [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Separation properties#_definition _ Hausdorff spaces, $T_{2}$ spaces|Hausdorff]], [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Separation properties#_definition _ regular spaces, $T_{3}$ spaces|regular]], and [[Linear algebra done right --- ladr/notes/Normal operators#_definition _ normal|normal]].

###### _proof:_

We've shown already that any metric space is normal —
![[Topology --- math-147/notes/Separation properties#_example _ any Mathematical Analysis I --- math-131/notes/Metric spaces _definition_ metric space, metric metric space is regular and normal|Separation properties]]

Note that any metric space is also $T_{1}$ — for any two points $x, y \in X$ with distance $d$ between them, choose balls of radius $d$ around them. Thus, $X$ is in fact $T_{4}$. [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Separation properties#_proposition _ hierarchy of separation properties|This implies]] $T_{3}$, $T_{2}$ and the other separation properties.

Metric spaces also satisfy an equivalence of various size restrictions and covering properties.

##### _proposition:_ equivalent size restrictions on a metric space

For a metric space $X$, the following are equivalent.
1) $X$ is [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Size restrictions#_definition _ separable|separable]].
2) $X$ is [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Size restrictions#_definition _ second countable|second countable]].
3) $X$ is Lindelöf.
4) Every uncountable set in $X$ has a limit point.

###### _proof:_

Suppose $X$ is separable. Let $A$ be a countable dense subset $A \subseteq X$. Then consider $\mathcal{B}$, the collection of all balls of rational radius centred at points in $A$. This is a countable collection. We claim it is a [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Bases#_definition _ basis|basis]] of the metric topology. For each point $p$ in an open set $U$, there is some radius $r$ with $B_{r / 2}(p) \subseteq B_{r}(p) \subseteq U$. Since $B_{r / 2}(p)$ is open and $A$ is dense, it contains a basic open ball $B_{\delta}(a)$ around some $a \in A$. Then $B_{\delta + q}(a) \subseteq B_{r}(p) \subseteq U$ is a basic open set containing $p$, where $q$ is a rational between $d(p, a)$ and $r / 2$. [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Bases#_proposition _ equivalent conditions to be a basis of a specific topology|This is sufficient]] to show $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis of the metric topology.

If $X$ is second countable, it has a countable basis. Each open set in the open cover is the union of basic open sets, and thus, reduces to a countable cover.

If $X$ is Lindelöf, every cover of $X$ has a countable subcover. This includes the cover by all balls. Each uncountable set must then include uncountably many points in some open ball. It then includes uncountably many points in the closure of the ball, which is [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Compactness#_definition _ compact|compact]]. By the [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Compactness#_theorem _ the Bolzano-Weierstrass theorem|Bolzano-Weierstrass theorem]], the uncountable set must have a limit point.

The proof that (4) $\implies$ (1) follows from using Zorn's lemma.
