There's a natural definition of a product of topological spaces — one that generalises the product of $\mathbb{R}$s to $\mathbb{R}^n$, and also the product metrics (that take a $p$-norm metric of the metrics on finitely many [[Mathematical Analysis I --- math-131/notes/Metric spaces#_definition_ metric space, metric|metric spaces]]). The first definition of a product generates its topology from rectangles (much like [[Calculus --- spivak/notes/Topological considerations#_definition _ open set|Spivak does]]).

##### _definition:_ (finite) product topology

Given topological spaces $X, Y$, the product topology on $X \times Y$ is the topology [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Bases#_definition _ basis|generated by]] all products of open sets $U \times V$ where $U \subseteq X, V \subseteq Y$.

Note that $X \times Y$ is open and any intersections of basic open sets are covered by the products of the corresponding open sets in $U$ and $V$ — 
$$
(U_{1} \times V_{1}) \cap (U_{2} \times V_{2}) = (U_{1} \cap U_{2}) \times (V_{1} \cap V_{2})
$$
so products of open sets [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Bases#_proposition _ equivalent conditions to be a basis of any topology|really do generate a topology]].

##### _example:_ open sets in the product topology

Note that open sets don't have to be products of open sets — open balls in $\mathbb{R}^{2}$ are not products of open intervals.

##### _proposition:_ products of closed sets are closed

If $C, D$ are [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Limit points and closed sets#_definition _ closure, closed|closed]] in topological spaces $X, Y$ respectively, then $C \times D$ is closed in the product topology on $X \times Y$.

###### _proof:_

We show that the complement ${X \times Y \setminus C \times D}$ is open. 

Notice that if $(x, y) \in C \times D$, we must have $x \in C$ and $y \in D$. Thus, the complement contains points where either the first or second component is not in $C$ or $D$ respectively. That is, the complement is the union of $(X \setminus C) \times Y$ and $X \times (Y \setminus D)$ (not just the product of the complements). But since $C, D$ are closed in $X, Y$ respectively, [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Limit points and closed sets#_theorem _ closed sets are complements of open sets|their complements are open]]. Since the whole sets $X, Y$ are open in themselves, the complement
$$
X \times Y \setminus C \times D = ((X \setminus C) \times Y) \cup (X \times (Y \setminus D))
$$
is the union of basic open sets.

We can also define products more categorically by using the natural projection functions on them. This definition generalises more naturally to infinite products. In fact, (though we won't show this yet) this definition makes clear that topological products satisfy the [[Algebraic geometry --- rising-sea/notes/Categories, functors, and natural transformations#_example _ product|universal property of a product]]!

##### _definition:_ (finite) projection functions

Given two sets $X, Y$, the projection functions $\pi_{X} : X \times Y \to X$ and $\pi_{Y} : X \times Y \to Y$ are given by $\pi_{X} : (x, y) \mapsto x$ and $\pi_{Y} : (x, y) \to y$.

##### _proposition:_ generating the product topology by pre-images of projections

The product topology on $X \times Y$ is generated by the [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Subbases#_definition _ subbasis|subbasis]] of inverse images of sets under the projection functions — the subbasis 
$$
\mathcal{S} = \{ \pi_{X}^\text{pre}(U) \mid U \in \mathcal{T}_{X} \} \cup \{ \pi_{Y}^\text{pre}(V) \mid V \in \mathcal{T}_{Y} \}.
$$

###### _proof sketch:_

Note that each element of $\mathcal{S}$ is either $U \times Y$ or $X \times V$ with $U, V$ open in $X, Y$ respectively. Thus, the subbasis is clearly a subset of the topology on $X \times Y$. The finite intersections of these subbasic open sets are just the basis of the product topology — sets $U \times V$ with $U, V$ open.

##### _example:_ $\mathbb{R}^{2}$ is $\mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R}$

Since every open disc contains an open rectangle around each of its points and every open rectangle contains an open disc around each of its points, the standard Euclidean topology on $\mathbb{R}^{2}$ generated by open discs and the product topology generated on $\mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R}$ by open rectangles are the same.

Finally, there's another way to understand the product topology on $X \times Y$ in terms of [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Continuous functions|continuous functions]]. $X \times Y$ is topologised with just enough open sets so that the projections to each factor are continuous. This is essentially equivalent to the [[Algebraic geometry --- rising-sea/notes/Categories, functors, and natural transformations#_example _ product|universal property of the product]]. 

##### _proposition:_ the product topology is the coarsest topology with continuous projections

The product topology is the [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Bases#_definition _ strength of a topology, coarser, finer|coarsest]] topology on the set $X \times Y$ such that the projection maps $\pi_{X}$ and $\pi_{Y}$ are continuous.

###### _proof sketch:_

We will show that any topology coarser than the product topology makes either $\pi_{X}$ or $\pi_{Y}$ not continuous. 

Suppose we have a topology $\mathcal{T}$ on $X \times Y$ that is coarser than the product topology. Then it must exclude some open set in the product topology. Since the topology generated by a [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Subbases#_definition _ subbasis|subbasis]] is the smallest topology containing the sets of the subbasis, $\mathcal{T}$ must specifically exclude one of the sets in the subbasis generating the product topology. Without loss of generality, it excludes $\pi_{X}^\text{pre}(U)$ for $U$ open in $X$. Then by definition, $\pi_{X}$ is not continuous.

### Infinite products

Since $(U \times V) \times W = U \times (V \times W)$ we can define the product topology on three, and thus, $n$ sets inductively as $(X \times Y) \times Z$. However, this doesn't get us infinite products.

To think of finite products we usually use the trick of encoding pairs $(x, y)$ as $\{ x, \{ y \} \}$ or something similar. However, this isn't as manageable for infinite products. Typically, we think of an infinite [[Mathematical Analysis I --- math-131/notes/Countability#_definition _ countability, finiteness|countable]] list as a [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Sequences, topologically#_definition _ sequence|sequence]] — a function $\mathbb{N} \to X$. We extend this idea to think of arbitrary infinite set theoretic products.

##### _definition:_ (arbitrary, set-theoretic) products

Let $\{ X_{\alpha} \}_{\alpha \in \mathcal{I}}$ be a collection of sets. The product of all of the sets is the set of functions
$$
\prod_{\alpha \in \mathcal{I}} X_{\alpha} = \left\{  x : \mathcal{I} \to \bigcup_{\alpha \in \mathcal{I}} X_{\alpha} \mid x(\alpha) \in X_{\alpha} \text{ for each } \alpha \in \mathcal{I} \right\}.
$$

$x(\alpha)$ is called the $\alpha$th coordinate or component of $x$. $X_{\alpha}$ are called factors of the product.

##### _definition:_ (arbitrary) projection functions

Given a collection of sets $\{ X_{\alpha} \}_{\alpha \in \mathcal{I}}$ and their product $X = \prod_{\alpha \in \mathcal{I}} X_{\alpha}$, the $\beta$th projection function is $\pi_{\beta} : X \to X_{\beta}$ by $\pi_{\beta}(x) = x(\beta)$ for each $x \in X$.

Note here that the two definitions we gave for finite products disagree here — the topology generated by arbitrary products of open sets is [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Bases#_definition _ strength of a topology, coarser, finer|finer]] than the subbasis of pre-images under projection functions. Specifically, since subbases only allow for finite intersections, the latter only allows restrictions to products of open proper subsets in finitely many components while the rest are the whole space. This will be the natural topology to put on the space (for categorical, as well as topological reasons). The topology generated by arbitrary products of open sets includes all of the open sets of the product topology as well as smaller open sets.

##### _definition:_ (arbitrary, topological) products

Given a collection of topological spaces $\{ X_{\alpha} \}_{\alpha \in \mathcal{I}}$, their product is the set-theoretic product of the underlying sets $\prod_{\alpha \in \mathcal{I}} X_{\alpha}$ with the topology generated by the [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Subbases#_definition _ subbasis|subbasis]] of pre-images of open sets under the projection function — that is,
$$
\mathcal{S} = \bigcup_{\alpha \in \mathcal{I}} \{ \pi_{\alpha}^\text{pre}(U) \mid U \in \mathcal{T}_{X_{\alpha}} \}.
$$

##### _proposition:_ the product topology is the finite gate topology

Given a collection of topological spaces $\{ X_{\alpha} \}_{\alpha \in \mathcal{I}}$, the product topology on $\prod_{\alpha \in \mathcal{I}} X_{\alpha}$ is the topology generated by the basis of products of open sets with all but finitely many the whole $\alpha$th factor —
$$
\mathcal{B} = \left\{  \prod_{\alpha \in \mathcal{I}} U_{\alpha} \mid U_{\alpha} \in \mathcal{T}_{X_{\alpha}} \text{ and } U_{\alpha} = X_{\alpha} \text{ for all but finitely many } \alpha \in \mathcal{I}  \right\}.
$$
###### _proof sketch:_

If $U_{\alpha_{1}}, \dots, U_{\alpha_{n}}$ are the finitely many $U_{\alpha}$ that are not their whole factor, then we can write $\prod_{\alpha \in \mathcal{I}} = U_{\alpha}$ as the intersection $\bigcap_{\alpha \in \mathcal{I}} \pi_{\alpha}^\text{pre}(U_{\alpha})$ and vice versa. Thus, the basis generated by the subbasis defining the product topology, and $\mathcal{B}$ are the same collection of open sets.

##### _example:_ the power set topology

For any set $A$, we can take the the product of the two point [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Topologies#_example _ the discrete and indiscrete topologies|discrete]] topological space $\{ 0, 1 \}$ indexed by $a \in A$. We call this $\{ 0, 1 \}^A$. Since the elements of the topological space are just functions $A \to \{ 0, 1 \}$, we can think of them as subsets of $A$. Thus, we denote this topological space $2^A$.

##### _example:_ closures in the power set topology of $\mathbb{R}$

In the product $2^\mathbb{R}$, the set $Z = \{ x : \mathbb{R} \to \{ 0, 1 \} \mid x^\text{img}(\mathbb{Q}) = \{ 0 \} \}$ is the set of all functions that are zero on the rationals, or equivalently, the set of all subsets of the irrational numbers. Each $x : \mathbb{R} \to \{ 0, 1 \}$ with $x(q) = 1$ (that is, each $x \not\in Z$) for some rational $q$, is contained in the basic open set $\pi_{q}^\text{pre}(\{ 1 \})$ — the open set of all $f : \mathbb{R} \to \{ 0, 1 \}$ with $f(q) = 1$. This open set by definition excludes all of $Z$ since the functions in $Z$ have their support contained in the irrationals. Thus, $Z$ has no limit points not in $Z$ and is its own closure.

### The box topology, and why it's bad

We could have extended the definition of the product of finitely many spaces to infinite products without the "finite gate" requirement — allowing infinitely many gates. While this does indeed define a topology, we will see that it is not very good.

##### _definition:_ box topology

Given a collection of topological spaces $\{ X_{\alpha} \}_{\alpha \in \mathcal{I}}$, the box topology on their product is the topology on $\prod_{\alpha \in \mathcal{I}} X_{\alpha}$ generated by the basis
$$
\mathcal{B} = \left\{  \prod_{\alpha \in \mathcal{I}} U_{\alpha} \mid U_{\alpha} \in \mathcal{T}_{X_{\alpha}}  \right\}.
$$

##### _example:_ the box topology is less natural than the product topology on $\mathbb{R}^\omega$

We can compare the box and product topologies on the countable product of $\mathbb{R}$. If $A$ is the subset consisting of all points with only positive coordinates, then $0 = (0, 0, \dots)$ is a [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Limit points and closed sets#_definition _ limit point|limit point]] of $A$ in both topologies, but only has a sequence in $A$ [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Sequences, topologically#_definition _ convergence|converging to it]] in the product topology.

In the box topology, every open set containing $0$ contains the product of open sets $U_{i}$ with $0 \in U_{i} \subseteq \mathbb{R}$. Each of these $U_{i}$ must contain some positive $x_{i} \in U_{i} \subseteq \mathbb{R}$. Thus, $(x_{1}, x_{2}, \dots) \in \prod_{i \in \mathbb{N}} U_{i}$. Since each open set containing $0$ contains elements of $A$, and $0 \not\in A$, $0$ [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Limit points and closed sets#_proposition _ limit points not in the set|is a limit point]] of $A$.

Since the box topology is finer than the product topology, $0$ must be a limit point of $A$ in the product topology too.

In the product topology, $\{ x_{n} = (1 / n, 1 / n, \dots) \}_{n \in \mathbb{N}} \subseteq A$ converges to $0$. Any open set $U$ containing $0$ contains $U_{i_{1}} \times \dots \times U_{i_{m}}$ for some finite $m$ and of $X_{i}$ in $i \neq i_{k}$. Choosing $N$ large enough so that $1 / N \in U_{i_{k}}$ for each $k$ (which we can do since there are only finitely many $U_{i_{k}}$), we get $x_{n} \in U$ for all $n > N$. [[Topology --- math-147/notes/Sequences, topologically#_definition _ convergence|By definition]] we have $x_{n} \to x$.

However, in the box topology, given any sequence $\{ x_{n} \}_{n \in \mathbb{N}} \subseteq A$, we can create an open set $U$ so that the $n$th component of $x_{n}$ is not in $U$. We choose $U_{n} = (-a_{n}, a_{n})$ where $a_{i} < \pi_{n}(x_{n})$ (note here that we're taking the $n$th component of $x_{n}$ which is a full element of the product and not just the $n$th component of an element). Then for $U = \prod_{n \in \mathbb{N}} U_{n}$, for each $n$ we have $x_{n} \not\in U$ and thus, we can't have any $N \in \mathbb{N}$ such that for all $n > N$, all $x_{n}$ are in $U$ — $x_{n}$ does not converge to $A$.

This example can be taken further by considering continuous functions into the box topology on $\mathbb{R}^\omega$. Because the box topology has extra open sets, functions that really should be continuous (and that are in the product topology) aren't continuous in the box topology. For example, the diagonal map $f : x \mapsto (x, x, \dots)$ is continuous in the product topology but not in the box topology for similar reasons to those above.

First suppose $\mathbb{R}^{\omega}$ is given the product topology. Then, for each $x \in \mathbb{R}$, each open neighbourhood $V$ of $f(x)$ contains a basic open set that is the product of finitely many boxes followed by the rest that are the whole factor space $\mathbb{R}$. That is, there is some basic open set
$$
\prod_{n = 1}^{N} (x - r_{n}, x + r_{n}) \times \prod_{n = N + 1}^{\infty} \mathbb{R}
$$
contained in $V$.

Since there are finitely many $r_{n}$, we can choose $r = \min \{ r_{n} \}$. Then the open neighbourhood $U = (x - r, x + r)$ of $x$ has image contained in the basic open set, and thus $V$ too. That is,
$$
f^{\text{img}}(U) = \prod_{n = 1}^{\infty} (x - r, x + r) \subseteq \prod_{n = 1}^{N} (x - r_{n}, x + r_{n}) \times \prod_{n = N + 1}^{\infty} \mathbb{R} \subseteq V.
$$

This shows that $f$ is continuous at each $x \in \mathbb{R}$, and thus, is continuous on all of  $X$.

Now suppose $\mathbb{R}^{\omega}$ is given the box topology. Consider the open neighbourhood
$$
V = \prod_{n = 1}^{\infty} (-1/n, 1/n)
$$
of $(0, 0, \dots)$. Notice that $f(0) = (0, 0, \dots)$. We claim that there is no open neighbourhood $U$ of $0$ such that $f^{\text{img}}(U) \subseteq V$. Thus, by theorem 7.1 (4), $f$ is not continuous.

By definition of the Euclidean topology on $\mathbb{R}$, any open neighbourhood $U$ of $0$ contains some open interval $(-r, r)$. However, by the Archimedean property of $\mathbb{R}$, there is some $n \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $1/n < r$. Thus, $f^{\text{img}}(-r, r) = \prod_{n = 1}^{\infty} (-r, r) \not\subseteq V$. Since $(-r, r) \subseteq U$, we have  $f^{\text{img}}(-r, r) \subseteq f^{\text{img}}(U)$ and thus, $f^{\text{img}}(U) \not\subseteq V$ as well.
