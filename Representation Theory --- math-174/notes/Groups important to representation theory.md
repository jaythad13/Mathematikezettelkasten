Representation theory involves lots of [[Abstract Algebra I --- math-171/notes/Groups, and why you should care#_definition _ group, identity, inverse|groups]], and some particular groups are very important. They are these.

### The general linear group

In representation theory, we're going to work a lot with linear representations. These are ways of embedding groups into the [[Abstract Algebra I --- math-171/notes/Matrix groups and the quaternions#_definition _ the general linear group, $ mathrm{GL}_{n}( mathbb{F})$|general linear group]] over some field. Thus, we want to understand these general linear groups.

In general (for fields of [[Algebraic Geometry --- math-176/notes/Abstract algebra review#_definition _ characteristic|characteristic]] $0$), $\operatorname{GL}_{n}(\mathbb{F})$ is huge. In particular, for $\mathbb{F} = \mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{F} =  \mathbb{C}$, it is uncountable and cannot be generated by a countable set. However, it does admit a reasonable set of generators. In particular, every invertible matrix (and equivalently, linear transformation) is generated by a bunch of elementary transformations.

##### _proposition:_ generators of $\operatorname{GL}_{n}$

The general linear group is generated by the set containing all $C_{ij}$ (the identity matrix with columns $i$ and $j$ swapped), $E_{ij}(\lambda)$ (the identity matrix with some scalar $\lambda$ in the $(i, j)$th entry), and $S_{i}(\lambda)$ (the identity matrix with the $i$th column scaled by some $\lambda$).

### Transformation groups

Transformation groups are really just groups automorphisms of a set. These are a generalisation of the finite groups which [[Abstract Algebra I --- math-171/notes/Group combinatorics and symmetric groups#_theorem _ Cayley's theorem|are subgroups of]] the finite symmetric groups $S_{n}$.

##### _definition:_ automorphism, automorphism group, transformation group

Given a set $X$, a automorphisms of $X$ is a bijection $f : X \to X$.

The group of all transformations under composition is the automorphism group of $X$, $\operatorname{Aut}_{\mathsf{Set}} X$. This is the same thing as [[Abstract Algebra I --- math-171/notes/Group combinatorics and symmetric groups#_definition _ the symmetric group on $ Omega$|the symmetric group]] or automorphism group or permutation group of an arbitrary set $X$.

A transformation group on $X$ is a subgroup of $\operatorname{Aut}_{\mathsf{Set}} X$.

Often the case that we are considered in is that where $X$ is an object in some (concrete) [[Basic category theory --- basic-cat/notes/Categories#_definition _ category|category]] $\mathscr{C}$ and the transformation group is the automorphism group of $X$ in that category — $\operatorname{Aut}_{\mathscr{C}} X \le \operatorname{Aut}_{\mathsf{Set}} X$.

##### _example:_ the general linear group is a transformation group

$\operatorname{GL}_{n}(\mathbb{F})$ is the group of [[Linear algebra done right --- ladr/notes/Vector spaces#_definition _ vector space|vector space]] automorphisms of $\mathbb{F}^n$.

### Symmetric groups

Most of the detailed theory we will explore is the representation theory of the symmetric group. This is the special case of the full subgroup of the automorphism group of a finite set. Note that here we're only interested in finite symmetric groups. Since finite sets are determined (for the purposes of category theory) entirely by their cardinality, we can define the symmetric group as the automorphisms of $[n] = \{ 1, \dots, n \}$.

##### _definition:_ symmetric group, permutation

The symmetric group on $n$ letters is
$$
S_{n} = \mathfrak{S}_{n} = \operatorname{Aut} [n].
$$

Each of its elements is called a permutation.
